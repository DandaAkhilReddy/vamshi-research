Introduction: Why 2026 Survey Optimization Matters

The 2026 MLS survey is critical for your thesis because it:

  • Completes the 3-year time series necessary for robust temporal stability analysis (Direction 2)
  • Provides validation dataset for suitability models developed from 2024-2025 data (Direction 1)
  • Improves change rate calculations by adding third data point for trend analysis (Directions 3, 4, 5)
  • Captures inter-annual variability - distinguishes persistent patterns from anomalies

⚠️ Critical: Ensure Temporal Consistency

For multi-temporal analysis, the 2026 survey must closely match 2024 and 2025 survey parameters: timing, tide conditions, lane positioning, and instrument settings. Inconsistency introduces confounding variables that complicate change detection.

1. Survey Timing Optimization

1.1 Seasonal Timing

Recommendation: Match 2024/2025 Survey Months

  • Verify: What months were 2024 and 2025 surveys conducted? (e.g., April? July? October?)
  • Schedule 2026: Within same 2-week window as previous years if possible
  • Rationale: Seasonal beach morphology varies (winter vs. summer profiles); consistent timing reduces seasonal signal in change detection
  • If exact timing impossible: Document offset and consider seasonal correction factors in analysis

1.2 Tidal Window Optimization

Recommendation: Low Tide Window ± 1 Hour for Maximum Beach Exposure

Parameter Target Specification Rationale
Tidal Stage Low tide ± 1 hour Maximizes exposed beach width; ensures consistent waterline position across years
Tide Height Within 0.2 m of 2024/2025 tide levels Minimizes horizontal waterline shift between surveys (~10m for 1:20 slope)
Avoid Spring tides (new/full moon ±2 days) Extreme highs/lows complicate comparison; neap tides preferable for consistency

Action Item:

  • Obtain NOAA tide predictions for PAIS (Port Aransas or Bob Hall Pier station)
  • Identify 3-5 candidate survey dates in target month with optimal tide windows
  • Build 2-day buffer for weather contingency

1.3 Weather and Environmental Conditions

Optimal Conditions for High-Quality MLS Data

  • Wind: < 10 mph ideal; < 15 mph acceptable (reduces sand drift obscuring surface)
  • Precipitation: No rain during survey or previous 24 hours (wet sand alters surface reflectivity)
  • Recent Storms: Avoid surveys immediately after major storms (allow 2+ weeks for beach stabilization unless intentionally capturing storm response)
  • Visibility: Clear to partly cloudy (improves georeferencing if using camera; not critical for LiDAR alone)

2. Survey Coverage and Lane Positioning

2.1 Alongshore Coverage

Recommendation: Match or Exceed 2024/2025 Extent + 500m Overlap

  • Minimum: Survey exact same beach segments as 2024/2025 (critical for change detection)
  • Preferred: Extend 250-500m beyond previous endpoints each direction
    • Rationale: Captures shoreline migration that may have shifted optimal nesting zones
    • Ensures no loss of coverage if endpoints have eroded
  • Documentation: Record GPS coordinates of survey start/end points for future surveys

2.2 Cross-Shore Lane Coverage

Critical: Consistent Lane Positioning

For temporal analysis, each year's survey should cover same cross-shore zone:

  • Seaward Extent: To waterline at time of survey (tide-dependent)
  • Landward Extent: To dune crest or vegetation line (whichever is farther)
  • Number of Passes: Match 2024/2025 (e.g., 3 passes: waterline, mid-beach, dune toe?)

Why this matters for your thesis:

Missing dune toe in one year but not others → cannot calculate dune migration (Direction 2, 3).
Waterline coverage inconsistent → beach width calculations unreliable (Direction 2, 4).

2.3 Lane Overlap for Seamless Coverage

Recommendation: 30-50% Sidelap Between Adjacent Lanes

Overlap % Pros Cons Recommendation
< 20% Faster survey Gaps in coverage, irregular point density Avoid
30-50% Seamless coverage, uniform density, some redundancy for QA Moderate time investment Optimal
> 60% Excellent redundancy Inefficient; excessive data processing Only if needed for complex topography

Implementation:

  • Velodyne VLP-16 typical swath width ~40m at 25m range
  • Lane spacing for 40% overlap = 24m between lane centerlines
  • Use GPS tracks from previous years as guides; pre-load into navigation system

2.4 Repeat Passes for Quality Assurance

Recommendation: Bi-Directional Passes + One Repeat Section

Strategy:

  1. Primary survey: South-to-north alongshore lanes
  2. Return pass: North-to-south (captures any missed gaps)
  3. QA repeat: Re-survey one 500m test section in opposite direction
    • Compare point clouds from both passes
    • Quantify precision: compute elevation RMSE between passes
    • If RMSE > 5cm → investigate instrument calibration or georeferencing issues

3. Data Quality Assurance Protocols

3.1 Real-Time Quality Checks During Survey

In-Field QA Checklist (Check After Each Lane)

  • ✓ GPS positional accuracy: HDOP < 2.0, > 8 satellites
  • ✓ IMU data recording properly (pitch, roll, yaw)
  • ✓ LiDAR points being collected (real-time display if available)
  • ✓ Point density appears consistent (no obvious gaps)
  • ✓ Lane coverage extends to planned seaward and landward limits
  • ✓ Timestamp synchronization between GPS, IMU, and LiDAR

3.2 Base Station Setup for RTK/PPK Accuracy

Critical: Consistent Base Station Location

Recommendation: Use same base station location as 2024/2025 surveys

  • Why: Minimizes relative horizontal/vertical errors between years
  • Specification:
    • Base station on known benchmark (same as previous years)
    • Occupation time: ≥ 2 hours for static baseline
    • Distance to survey area: < 10 km preferred (accuracy degrades with distance)
  • Documentation: Record base station coordinates, antenna height, occupation duration

Alternative if RTK unavailable:

Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK) using CORS stations (continuously operating reference stations). NOAA CORS network has stations along Texas coast (nearest: TXPR, TXCI). Download RINEX data for survey day and post-process trajectories.

3.3 Instrument Calibration and Settings

Pre-Survey Calibration Checklist

Component Calibration Task Frequency
LiDAR Scanner Range calibration against known targets Before each field campaign
GPS/IMU Boresight Lever-arm offsets and angular alignment After any equipment remounting
GPS Antenna Antenna height measurement (to ARP) Each survey day
IMU Static alignment (level platform, 5+ min) Each survey day before first line

Recommended Instrument Settings (Velodyne VLP-16 or Similar)

  • Rotation Rate: 10-20 Hz (higher = denser cross-track spacing)
  • Dual Return Mode: Enable (captures ground through vegetation)
  • GPS Update Rate: 1-10 Hz (match or exceed LiDAR scan rate)
  • Vehicle Speed: 15-25 mph (slower = higher point density but longer survey time)

3.4 Post-Survey Data Validation

Same-Day QA/QC (Before Leaving Field Site)

  1. Data Transfer: Copy raw LiDAR, GPS, IMU files to backup drive (redundancy!)
  2. File Integrity: Verify all files complete, no corruption
  3. Coverage Check: Load point cloud in CloudCompare or viewer → visual inspection for gaps
  4. Trajectory Quality: Plot GPS tracks; check for positional jumps or dropouts
  5. Benchmark Checks: If time permits, survey 2-3 stable ground control points (GCPs)
    • Examples: Building corners, road intersections, permanent markers
    • Compare 2026 elevation to 2024/2025 values → should be identical (± 5cm)
    • Significant differences indicate georeferencing error

4. Special Considerations for Thesis Objectives

4.1 For Microtopography Analysis (Direction 1)

  • Slow down in nesting zones: Reduce vehicle speed to 10-15 mph in known high-density nest areas → increases point density for fine-scale texture
  • Multiple passes at different angles: If time permits, one cross-shore pass in addition to alongshore → improves surface normal calculations
  • Avoid disturbing nests: Survey before or after peak nesting season if microtopography is priority (no vehicle tracks altering surface)

4.2 For Temporal Stability Analysis (Direction 2 - RECOMMENDED)

Highest Priority: Temporal Consistency

  • ✓ Survey same calendar month as 2024/2025 (± 2 weeks max)
  • ✓ Match tide stage (low tide ± 1 hr) to within 0.2m of previous years
  • ✓ Same lane pattern (waterline, mid-beach, dune toe)
  • ✓ Same vehicle/instrument mounting to avoid lever-arm differences
  • ✓ Document any deviations from previous protocol → adjust analysis accordingly

4.3 For Dune Monitoring (Directions 2, 3, 5)

  • Ensure dune crest and toe coverage: Landward extent must reach beyond dune crest into back-dune swale
  • Vegetation penetration: Dual-return mode critical for ground detection under dune vegetation
  • Supplement with RTK transects: Hand-held RTK survey of dune toe and crest features (5-10 transects) for validation

4.4 For Predictive Modeling (Direction 4)

  • Accuracy over coverage: Prioritize georeferencing precision (RTK/PPK) over areal extent
  • Document survey conditions: Tide level, recent storm history, nourishment activities → these affect rate calculations
  • Include stable reference surfaces: Survey parking lots, roads, building pads → use as QA checkpoints (should show zero change)

5. Field Campaign Checklist

Pre-Survey (1 Week Before)

  • Confirm survey date matches seasonal timing of 2024/2025 surveys
  • Download NOAA tide predictions; identify optimal low tide windows
  • Check weather forecast; have backup date planned
  • Instrument calibration completed (boresight, range check)
  • GPS base station location identified (same as previous years)
  • Load previous survey GPS tracks into navigation system
  • PAIS permits/permissions confirmed
  • Field team briefed on QA protocols

Day of Survey

  • Base station setup on benchmark; 2+ hour occupation initiated
  • Mobile system powered; IMU static alignment (5 min)
  • GPS showing HDOP < 2.0, > 8 satellites
  • Real-time data logging verified (LiDAR, GPS, IMU all recording)
  • Tide stage confirmed within target window (predicted ± actual)
  • Weather conditions acceptable (wind < 15 mph, no rain)
  • Survey lanes cover waterline to dune crest
  • 30-50% sidelap maintained between lanes
  • After each lane: verify GPS/IMU quality, point cloud density
  • Repeat pass of 500m test section completed
  • RTK transects at dune toe/crest (if supplementing MLS)
  • Stable GCP checkpoints surveyed (2-3 points minimum)
  • Data copied to backup drive before leaving field
  • Coverage gaps identified → re-survey if time permits

Post-Survey (Same Day)

  • File integrity check (no corruption)
  • Point cloud preview in CloudCompare (coverage, density)
  • GPS trajectory quality review (no major jumps/gaps)
  • Base station data downloaded; occupation time documented
  • Survey metadata log completed (tide, weather, deviations from plan)
  • All data backed up to 2+ locations (field drive + lab server)

Data Processing (Within 1 Week)

  • GPS/IMU trajectory post-processed (PPK if using CORS)
  • Point cloud georeferenced and classified (ground vs. vegetation)
  • GCP comparison: 2026 elevations vs. 2024/2025 (RMSE < 5cm target)
  • Repeat pass comparison: precision assessment (self-consistency RMSE)
  • DEM generation (1m resolution) for immediate comparison to previous years
  • Preliminary change detection (visual DoD) to verify data quality
  • If major issues detected → plan re-survey while field window still open

7. Contingency Planning

What If... Scenarios

Weather forces survey delay beyond optimal window?

  • Proceed with survey; document offset from 2024/2025 timing
  • Acknowledge seasonal differences in thesis discussion
  • Focus analysis on metrics less affected by season (e.g., dune migration vs. beach width)

Equipment malfunction prevents full coverage?

  • Prioritize core nesting zone (central 60% of study area)
  • Ensure dune and shoreline features captured even if mid-beach gaps exist
  • Use 2025 data to interpolate missing 2026 zones if necessary

Point density lower than expected?

  • Acceptable for most objectives as long as > 10 pts/m² achieved
  • May limit microtopography analysis (Direction 1) but other directions unaffected

Georeferencing errors detected in post-processing?

  • Use GCPs for manual bias correction if errors systematic (uniform shift)
  • If errors non-systematic → may require re-survey or reduce precision claims in thesis

9. Summary: Key Priorities for 2026 Survey

Top 5 Priorities (in Order)

  1. Temporal Consistency: Match 2024/2025 timing, tide, lane pattern, and extent
  2. Complete Coverage: No gaps in alongshore or cross-shore coverage relative to previous years
  3. Georeferencing Accuracy: RTK/PPK with ≤ 5cm horizontal/vertical error
  4. Quality Assurance: Real-time checks, repeat passes, GCP validation
  5. Comprehensive Documentation: Metadata for interpreting any anomalies in change detection
