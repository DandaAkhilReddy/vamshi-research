Seasonal Dynamics During Incubation

The 45-60 day incubation period for Kemp's ridley sea turtles (May-June nesting ‚Üí August-September hatching) coincides with:

PAIS-Specific Challenges

A 2024 paper analyzing PAIS conservation strategies concluded that the population exhibits "stable or increasing" trends with laboratory incubation vs. "stable or decreasing" without it. The primary threats requiring ex-situ intervention are:

  • Beach driving: Vehicle compaction and direct nest destruction
  • Predation: Coyotes, ghost crabs, avian predators
  • Inundation: Storm tides and seasonal flooding
  • Erosion: Nest exposure and egg desiccation

üéØ Transition Pathway Identified

The literature supports a phased transition to in-situ incubation contingent on identifying and protecting "safe zones" that meet all stability criteria. Pilot studies should test protected in-situ sites before full-scale implementation.

"The Dominican Republic leatherback study demonstrates that when erosion and predation are controlled, in-situ success (74-85%) substantially exceeds hatchery outcomes (34-58%). This suggests that identifying naturally stable zones at PAIS could enable successful in-situ incubation without infrastructure costs."

3. Vehicle Impact on Coastal Morphology and Nesting

Direct Physical Impacts

Beach driving alters both physical habitat characteristics and nesting behavior:

  • Sand compaction: Reduced gas exchange decreases hatching success
  • Tire ruts: Hatchlings become trapped, increasing predation exposure time
  • Surface disruption: Alteration of moisture gradients and temperature profiles
  • False crawls: Higher rates on driven beaches as females avoid compacted zones

Morphological Consequences

Repeated vehicle traffic creates cumulative morphological changes:

  • Parallel tire corridors visible in LiDAR intensity data
  • Lower incubation temperatures in compacted zones (fewer female hatchlings due to temperature-dependent sex determination)
  • Increased local erosion susceptibility in disturbed sediments

4. Nest Elevation Preferences and Site Selection

Species-Specific Elevation Ranges

Sea turtles exhibit species-specific nest elevation preferences that balance flooding risk with desiccation risk:

  • Loggerheads: Prefer higher elevations with sparse vegetation
  • Greens: Mid-beach elevations with moderate vegetation
  • Kemp's ridley: Optimal range 1.5-4.0m above mean high water (MHW)

Slope as Primary Determinant

Recent research identifies slope as the greatest influence on nest site selection, with elevation being a correlated secondary factor. Kemp's ridley documented preferences:

  • Slope range: 3.5-6.7¬∞ (optimal nesting success)
  • Elevation range: 1.5-4.0m above MHW
  • Beach width: Minimum 30m from waterline to dune

‚öôÔ∏è LiDAR-Derived Metrics

MLS DEMs enable precise extraction of:

  • Elevation above MHW (using RTK-GPS tide datums)
  • Slope (via ArcGIS Pro Slope tool from DEM)
  • Beach width (distance between waterline and dune toe)
  • Nest-scale morphological variability (50m buffer statistics)

Flooding vs. Desiccation Trade-Off

Nests must be placed at elevations that avoid both extremes:

  • Too low (<1.5m): Flooding risk from storm tides and king tides during incubation
  • Too high (>4.0m): Desiccation risk, excessive temperatures, reduced moisture
  • Optimal zone (1.5-4.0m): Stable moisture, moderate temperatures, flood safety
"Slope appears to be the greatest influence on nest site selection, with elevation being a correlated factor. Female turtles assess beach profile suitability through slope perception, which correlates with elevation zones that balance moisture and flood risk."

5. Multi-Temporal Stability Classification Methods

Raster-Based Temporal Analysis

State-of-the-art methods for classifying coastal morphological stability using time-series LiDAR data:

  • Per-cell statistics: Calculate mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation (CV) across stacked DEMs
  • Change detection: DEMs of Difference (DoD) between survey epochs
  • Threshold-based classification: Define stability classes based on CV or absolute change
  • Spatial autocorrelation: Identify persistent vs. transitional zones

Recommended Workflow for PAIS

Using ArcGIS Pro with 2024, 2025, 2026 MLS DEMs:

  1. Stack DEMs: Ensure consistent spatial extent and resolution (0.1m)
  2. Cell Statistics tool: Calculate mean elevation and standard deviation per pixel
  3. Raster Calculator: CV = (Std Dev / Mean) √ó 100
  4. Reclassify: Define stability classes:
    • Highly Stable: CV < 10%
    • Moderately Stable: CV 10-30%
    • Dynamic: CV 30-50%
    • Highly Dynamic: CV > 50%
