1Beach Microtopographic Suitability Index for Kemp's Ridley Nesting

Integrating fine-scale surface texture and roughness with macro-scale geomorphology to enhance nest site selection models

Primary Research Question: How does fine-scale beach microtopography (surface roughness, texture, micro-slope variability) influence Kemp's ridley nest site selection beyond macro-scale parameters (beach width, slope, elevation), and can integration of micro- and macro-scale metrics improve habitat suitability predictions?

Novelty and Differentiation from RA Project

Why This Is Different

  • RA Project Focus: SLR vulnerability and shoreline change at landscape scale
  • Thesis Focus: Fine-scale substrate quality and microtopographic features at the <1m scale influencing individual nest site selection
  • Novel Contribution: First study to quantify beach microtopography metrics for sea turtle nesting habitat; builds on Figlus et al. (2020) by adding texture/roughness dimension

Research Gap Addressed

Existing studies use macro-parameters (slope, elevation, distance-to-water). Microtopographic variability at cm-scale—which may affect substrate compaction, digging effort, micro-drainage, and nest chamber stability—has never been quantified for sea turtle nesting beaches despite LiDAR capability to capture this detail.

Specific Datasets and Metrics

Primary Data Sources:

  • MLS Point Clouds: 2024, 2025, 2026 (700,000 pts/sec, ±2cm accuracy)
  • MLS-Derived DEMs: 10cm, 25cm, 50cm, 1m resolution (multi-scale analysis)
  • Historical Nest Locations: GPS coordinates from PAIS NPS archives
  • Beach Polygons: Delineated active nesting zones

Metrics to Derive:

Surface Roughness (SR)

Standard deviation of surface normals within moving window (1m, 3m, 5m radii)

Terrain Ruggedness Index (TRI)

Mean elevation difference between cell and 8 neighbors

Micro-Slope Variability (MSV)

Coefficient of variation of slope within focal windows

Surface Texture Index (STI)

Gray-level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM) from DEM as raster

Curvature Metrics

Profile curvature, planform curvature, total curvature

Macro-Parameters

Beach slope, elevation, width, distance to shoreline (from Figlus et al. 2020)

Step-by-Step Workflow

Phase 1: Data Preparation (ArcGIS Pro + CloudCompare)

Step 1.1: Import MLS point clouds (2024, 2025, 2026) into CloudCompare
  • Classify ground points (CSF algorithm or manual classification)
  • Remove noise (statistical outlier removal)
  • Compute point normals (radius = 0.5m)
Step 1.2: Generate multi-resolution DEMs in ArcGIS Pro
  • LAS Dataset to Raster: 10cm, 25cm, 50cm, 1m resolutions
  • Interpolation method: Natural Neighbor or IDW
  • Clip to active nesting zone polygons

Phase 2: Microtopographic Metric Calculation

Step 2.1: Surface Roughness (Spatial Analyst)
  • Focal Statistics → Standard Deviation → Neighborhood: Circle (3m radius)
  • Repeat for 1m and 5m radii
  • Result: SR_1m, SR_3m, SR_5m rasters
Step 2.2: Terrain Ruggedness Index
  • Use TRI tool in Spatial Analyst or Geomorphometry & Gradient Metrics Toolbox
  • Alternatively: Focal Statistics → Range → 3×3 neighborhood
Step 2.3: Micro-Slope Variability
  • Spatial Analyst → Slope (from 10cm DEM)
  • Focal Statistics → Std Dev of slope → 3m radius
  • Raster Calculator: MSV = (Std_Slope / Mean_Slope) × 100
Step 2.4: Curvature Analysis
  • Spatial Analyst → Curvature → Profile, Planform, and Total
  • Creates three curvature rasters
Step 2.5: Surface Texture Index (Advanced - Optional)
  • Convert DEM to 8-bit grayscale image
  • Use GLCM Texture tool (if available) or calculate in CloudCompare
  • Metrics: contrast, homogeneity, entropy

Phase 3: Macro-Parameter Calculation

Step 3.1: Beach Slope (Spatial Analyst → Slope from 1m DEM)
Step 3.2: Elevation (Direct from DEM)
Step 3.3: Beach Width
  • Digitize shoreline (mean high water) and dune toe from orthoimagery/DEM
  • Generate transects perpendicular to shoreline (every 10m)
  • Measure distance between shoreline and dune toe
  • Interpolate width to raster
Step 3.4: Distance to Shoreline
  • Euclidean Distance tool from shoreline polyline

Phase 4: Nest Location Analysis

Step 4.1: Import historical nest GPS points (shapefile or CSV)
Step 4.2: Extract metric values to nest points
  • Spatial Analyst → Extract Multi Values to Points
  • Extract all microtopographic and macro metrics to each nest point
Step 4.3: Generate random non-nest points (equal number)
  • Create Random Points tool within nesting zone but excluding buffer around actual nests (50m)
  • Extract same metrics to random points

Phase 5: Statistical Comparison (Excel or ArcGIS Tables)

Step 5.1: Compare nest vs. non-nest locations
  • Calculate mean, median, std dev for each metric
  • Perform t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests
  • Identify which microtopographic metrics significantly differ

Phase 6: Suitability Index Development

Step 6.1: Normalize all metrics to 0-1 scale
  • Raster Calculator: (Raster - Min) / (Max - Min)
Step 6.2: Weighted overlay based on statistical significance
  • Assign higher weights to metrics with strong nest association
  • Example: Beach_Slope (25%), Elevation (20%), SR_3m (15%), TRI (10%), MSV (10%), Distance (20%)
  • Use Weighted Overlay tool or Raster Calculator
Step 6.3: Generate Beach Microtopographic Suitability Index (BMSI) map
  • Result: Continuous suitability score 0-100
  • Classify: Unsuitable (0-20), Low (20-40), Moderate (40-60), High (60-80), Optimal (80-100)

Phase 7: Validation with 2026 Nest Data

Step 7.1: Apply developed suitability index to 2026 DEM
Step 7.2: Overlay 2026 nest locations
Step 7.3: Calculate validation metrics
  • What % of 2026 nests fall in High/Optimal zones?
  • Compare to random expectation
  • Assess model predictive accuracy

Expected Outputs and Deliverables

Maps:

  • Surface Roughness maps (3 scales: 1m, 3m, 5m)
  • Terrain Ruggedness Index map
  • Micro-Slope Variability map
  • Curvature maps (profile, planform, total)
  • Traditional macro-parameter maps (slope, elevation, beach width, distance to water)
  • Final Beach Microtopographic Suitability Index (BMSI) map for 2024, 2025, 2026
  • Change in BMSI 2024-2026 (areas improving/degrading)

Tables:

  • Comparative statistics: nest vs. non-nest locations for all metrics
  • Statistical significance tests (p-values, effect sizes)
  • Suitability zone area calculations (ha of each class)
  • Validation results (2026 nest location vs. predicted suitability)

Figures for Thesis:

  1. Study area map with 2024-2026 nest locations
  2. Multi-scale surface roughness comparison panels
  3. Box plots: nest vs. non-nest metric distributions
  4. Final BMSI map with nest overlay
  5. Temporal change in BMSI (2024-2026)
  6. Validation scatter plot: predicted suitability vs. actual nest density

How 2026 MLS Dataset Improves Results

  • Temporal validation: 2026 nests provide independent test dataset for model accuracy
  • Inter-annual variability: Assesses whether microtopography-suitability relationships are consistent or change seasonally/annually
  • Change detection: Identifies zones where microtopographic quality degrades, potentially explaining nest site shifts
  • Refinement: Can recalibrate suitability weights if 2026 patterns differ from 2024-2025

Limitations and Considerations

Potential Limitations:

  • No biological data: Cannot directly measure substrate compaction, moisture, or temperature—only proxy indicators
  • Correlation not causation: Microtopography associated with nests may be correlative, not causal
  • Historical nest data quality: GPS accuracy of archived nest locations may vary (±3-10m)
  • Temporal mismatch: Nest locations from multiple years; microtopography from 2024-2026
  • Beach dynamics: Microtopography changes rapidly; snapshot surveys may miss ephemeral features

Mitigation Strategies:

  • Use only high-accuracy GPS nest data (post-2015 with DGPS/RTK)
  • Acknowledge proxies in interpretation; recommend future field validation
  • Focus analysis on beach zones with lower dynamics (mid-to-upper beach)
  • Use multi-year composite analysis to identify persistent microtopographic patterns

Publication Venues

  • Remote Sensing - methodological focus on LiDAR texture analysis
  • Frontiers in Marine Science - follow-up to Figlus et al. (2020)
  • Journal of Coastal Research - beach geomorphology application
  • Chelonian Conservation and Biology - sea turtle habitat focus
  • ASPRS Annual Conference - geospatial methods

2Temporal Morphological Stability Mapping for Persistent Habitat Identification

Quantifying which beach segments maintain stable, optimal nesting conditions across multi-year MLS time series

Primary Research Question: Which beach segments at PAIS demonstrate temporal stability in geomorphic characteristics critical for Kemp's ridley nesting (slope, elevation, beach width), and can a Morphological Stability Index predict persistent vs. ephemeral high-quality habitat zones?

Novelty and Differentiation from RA Project

Why This Is Different

  • RA Project Focus: Quantifies SLR vulnerability and long-term shoreline migration trends
  • Thesis Focus: Identifies temporally stable habitat zones for targeted conservation vs. ephemeral zones requiring dynamic management
  • Novel Contribution: First multi-temporal assessment of nesting habitat stability for sea turtles using high-frequency LiDAR monitoring

Management Value

PAIS managers can focus intensive monitoring and protection efforts on stable, persistent habitat zones while developing adaptive strategies for dynamic zones. This informs nest relocation priorities and beach nourishment site selection.

Specific Datasets and Metrics

Primary Data:

  • MLS DEMs: 2024, 2025, 2026 (1m resolution)
  • Derived shorelines (mean high water) for each year
  • Derived dune toe positions for each year
  • Historical nest density maps (nests per 100m beach segment)

Metrics to Calculate:

Elevation Stability (ES)

Coefficient of Variation (CV) of elevation across 3 years per pixel

Slope Stability (SS)

CV of slope across 3 years

Beach Width Stability (BWS)

CV of beach width along transects

Morphological Change Magnitude (MCM)

Mean absolute elevation change (2024-2025, 2025-2026)

Dune Toe Migration Rate (DTMR)

Annual dune toe position change (m/yr)

Composite Stability Index (CSI)

Weighted combination of all stability metrics

Step-by-Step Workflow

Phase 1: Multi-Temporal DEM Preparation

Step 1.1: Co-register 2024, 2025, 2026 DEMs
  • Ensure all DEMs in same coordinate system and resolution (1m)
  • Check horizontal and vertical alignment using stable features (buildings, roads)
  • Apply adjustments if needed
Step 1.2: Define analysis zone
  • Clip to active nesting zone (from shoreline to dune crest)
  • Exclude non-beach features (parking lots, infrastructure)

Phase 2: Temporal Variability Metrics

Step 2.1: Elevation Stability
  • Stack 3 DEM rasters in ArcGIS Pro Composite Bands tool
  • Use Cell Statistics: MEAN, STD
  • Raster Calculator: ES = (STD / MEAN) × 100
  • Result: Lower ES = more stable elevation
Step 2.2: Slope Stability
  • Calculate slope for each DEM (Spatial Analyst → Slope)
  • Stack 3 slope rasters
  • Cell Statistics: MEAN, STD
  • SS = (STD / MEAN) × 100
Step 2.3: Beach Width Stability
  • Extract shoreline and dune toe for each year
  • Generate perpendicular transects every 25m along shore
  • Intersect transects with shoreline and dune toe; measure width
  • For each transect: calculate CV of width across 3 years
  • Interpolate transect CV values to raster
Step 2.4: Morphological Change Magnitude
  • DEM_2025 - DEM_2024 = Change1
  • DEM_2026 - DEM_2025 = Change2
  • Absolute values: ABS(Change1), ABS(Change2)
  • MCM = (ABS_Change1 + ABS_Change2) / 2
Step 2.5: Dune Toe Migration Rate
  • Digitize dune toe polyline for 2024, 2025, 2026
  • DSAS (Digital Shoreline Analysis System) or manual transect method
  • Calculate linear regression rate (m/yr) for each transect

Phase 3: Composite Stability Index

Step 3.1: Normalize all metrics to 0-1 scale
  • Invert metrics where lower = better stability (e.g., ES, SS, MCM)
  • StabilityMetric = 1 - [(Value - Min) / (Max - Min)]
Step 3.2: Weighted overlay
  • Example weights: ES (30%), SS (25%), BWS (20%), MCM (15%), DTMR (10%)
  • CSI = Weighted Sum of normalized metrics
  • Result: CSI ranges 0-100 (higher = more stable)
Step 3.3: Classify stability zones
  • Very Stable: CSI > 80
  • Stable: CSI 60-80
  • Moderately Stable: CSI 40-60
  • Dynamic: CSI 20-40
  • Highly Dynamic: CSI < 20

Phase 4: Nest Location Overlay

Step 4.1: Create nest density raster
  • Point to Raster or Kernel Density from nest points
  • Units: nests per 100m beach segment
Step 4.2: Cross-tabulate CSI vs. nest density
  • Zonal Statistics: Mean nest density per CSI class
  • Test hypothesis: Do stable zones have higher nest density?

Phase 5: Persistent Habitat Mapping

Step 5.1: Apply Figlus et al. (2020) suitability criteria
  • Identify optimal geomorphic thresholds for Kemp's ridley
  • Map suitable zones for 2024, 2025, 2026 separately
Step 5.2: Persistent habitat overlay
  • Raster Calculator: Areas suitable in ALL 3 years = Persistent Habitat
  • Areas suitable 2 of 3 years = Intermittent Habitat
  • Areas suitable 1 of 3 years = Ephemeral Habitat
Step 5.3: Combine with CSI
  • Priority Class 1: Persistent Habitat + Very Stable/Stable CSI
  • Priority Class 2: Persistent Habitat + Moderate CSI
  • Priority Class 3: Intermittent Habitat + Stable CSI
  • Priority Class 4: All other combinations

Expected Outputs

Maps:

  • Individual stability metric maps (ES, SS, BWS, MCM, DTMR)
  • Composite Stability Index (CSI) map with 5 classes
  • Persistent vs. Ephemeral Habitat map
  • Conservation Priority Zones map (4-class system)
  • Nest density overlay on CSI zones
  • Temporal change maps (zones becoming more/less stable)

Quantitative Metrics:

  • Area (ha) of each stability class
  • % of total nesting zone in each class
  • Mean nest density per stability class
  • Statistical tests (ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis): nest density vs. stability

Management Deliverables:

  • Ranked list of beach segments for focused protection
  • Recommendation: concentrate nest monitoring in Priority 1 zones
  • Adaptive management zones: Dynamic areas requiring flexible strategies

2026 Data Value

  • Completes 3-year time series necessary for robust stability metrics
  • Validates stability classifications (are "stable" zones still stable in 2026?)
  • Enables trend analysis (linear vs. cyclic variability)
  • Provides 2026 nest data to test whether stable zones attract more nests over time

Limitations

  • 3-year window relatively short for "stability" assessment (ideally 5-10 years)
  • Assumes stability patterns continue beyond 2026 (may not hold under accelerating SLR)
  • Single-season snapshots may miss intra-annual dynamics
  • Recommendation: Framework designed for future MLS surveys to extend time series

Publication Venues

  • Geomorphology - morphodynamic classification methods
  • Journal of Coastal Research - beach stability analysis
  • Marine Turtle Newsletter - management application
  • Ecological Indicators - stability index development

3Dune-Beach Vulnerability Index (DBVI) for Habitat Resilience Assessment

Integrating traditional coastal vulnerability metrics with species-specific habitat requirements to assess long-term nesting habitat resilience

Primary Research Question: Can a habitat-specific vulnerability index combining coastal geomorphic vulnerability (erosion, SLR susceptibility, dune condition) with Kemp's ridley nesting requirements identify beach segments at risk of habitat degradation versus those with long-term resilience?

Novelty and Differentiation

Why This Is Different from RA Project

RA Project: General SLR vulnerability for entire PAIS coastline
This Thesis: Habitat-specific vulnerability focused on nesting zones, integrating biological requirements with physical vulnerability

Novel Contribution

First vulnerability index tailored to wildlife habitat (vs. infrastructure, human communities). Answers: "Where will suitable habitat persist despite coastal change?" rather than just "Where will erosion occur?"

DBVI Components

Component Metric Data Source Why It Matters
C1: Erosion Rate Shoreline change rate (m/yr) MLS-derived shorelines 2024-2026 Erosion reduces nesting area
C2: Beach Width Current width + trend MLS DEMs Narrow beaches unsuitable
C3: Elevation Mean beach elevation MLS DEMs Inundation risk under SLR
C4: Dune Height Dune crest - beach elevation MLS DEMs Storm protection buffering
C5: Dune Toe Erosion Dune toe retreat rate Multi-temporal dune toe lines Loss of back-beach nesting zones
C6: Slope Suitability Deviation from optimal (3.5-6.7°) MLS slope analysis Nest site selection criterion
C7: SLR Exposure % area inundated at +1m SLR NOAA projections + DEM Long-term habitat loss
C8: Habitat Stability From Direction 2 CSI Multi-temporal MLS Temporal habitat persistence

Workflow

Phase 1: Calculate Individual Components

C1 - Erosion Rate:
  • DSAS tool or manual shoreline change analysis
  • Calculate End Point Rate (EPR) or Linear Regression Rate (LRR)
C2 - Beach Width:
  • Transect-based measurement for each year
  • Width trend: linear regression slope
C3 - Elevation:
  • Zonal Statistics: mean elevation within nesting zone polygons
C4 - Dune Height:
  • Extract dune crest elevation (local maxima from DEM)
  • Subtract mean beach elevation
C5 - Dune Toe Erosion:
  • From dune toe migration analysis (Direction 2)
C6 - Slope Suitability:
  • Optimal range: 3.5-6.7°
  • Deviation score: ABS(Slope - Midpoint(5.1°))
  • Invert: Lower deviation = higher suitability
C7 - SLR Exposure:
  • Add 1m to current mean high water elevation
  • Con tool: Areas below new waterline = inundated
  • Calculate % of beach segment inundated
C8 - Habitat Stability:
  • Use CSI from Direction 2

Phase 2: Standardize and Invert Components

Standardization: Normalize all to 0-1 scale
Inversion: Ensure higher values = LOWER vulnerability
  • Low erosion rate → High resilience score
  • High dune height → High resilience score
  • Low SLR exposure → High resilience score

Phase 3: Calculate DBVI

Weighted Overlay:

Example weights (adjust based on expert input):

  • C1 Erosion: 20%
  • C2 Beach Width: 15%
  • C3 Elevation: 10%
  • C4 Dune Height: 15%
  • C5 Dune Erosion: 10%
  • C6 Slope: 10%
  • C7 SLR: 10%
  • C8 Stability: 10%
DBVI Calculation:

DBVI = Σ(Component × Weight)

Result: Scale 0-100 (higher = MORE resilient)

Phase 4: Classification

Resilience Classes:
  • Very High Resilience: DBVI > 80
  • High Resilience: 60-80
  • Moderate Resilience: 40-60
  • Low Resilience: 20-40
  • Very Low Resilience: < 20

Expected Outputs

Maps:

  • All 8 individual component maps
  • Final DBVI map (5-class resilience zones)
  • Priority conservation zones (High/Very High Resilience + High nest density)
  • At-risk zones (Low/Very Low Resilience + current nesting activity)

Management Tools:

  • Segment-level DBVI scores and rankings
  • Recommendations for beach nourishment site selection
  • Nest relocation priority matrix

2026 Contribution

Complete 3-year trend data for erosion, width change, dune migration improves rate calculations. Validates DBVI: do "resilient" zones maintain quality in 2026?

Limitations

  • Subjective weight assignment (can conduct sensitivity analysis)
  • Static SLR scenario (could run multiple scenarios)
  • Doesn't account for storm events (add storm frequency as optional component)

Publication Venues

  • Coastal Engineering - vulnerability index methods
  • Ocean & Coastal Management - management application
  • Endangered Species Research - conservation focus
