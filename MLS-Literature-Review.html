1. Mobile LiDAR in Coastal Geomorphology (2020-2025)

1.1 Technology and Platform Capabilities

Mobile LiDAR Performance Characteristics

Mobile LiDAR systems integrated with GPS and IMU hardware can scan approximately 10 km of beach and cliff with high spatial resolution (~1000 pts/m²) in just a few hours. UAV LiDAR systems achieve vertical accuracy of approximately 5 cm, while ground-based mobile systems demonstrate ±2 cm accuracy at 25 m collecting about 700,000 points per second.

The Coastal Processes Group uses LiDAR scanners mounted on trucks, drones, all-terrain vehicles, buildings, and towers to measure waves, beach erosion, and cliff erosion. This multi-platform approach enables comprehensive coastal monitoring across different scales and environments.

1.2 Accuracy Assessment at Padre Island

USGS Accuracy Assessment Study (Padre Island National Seashore): Final point cloud accuracy of 0.060 m (east), 0.095 m (north), and 0.053 m (height). Mobile terrestrial LiDAR proven useful for mapping complex features like dunes and monitoring geomorphic changes.

A recent Hurricane Hanna impact assessment study (2020) utilized a Velodyne mobile LiDAR system integrated with a 360-degree spherical camera along North Padre Island beach. This rapid assessment approach detected elevation change following the storm, demonstrating the value of mobile systems for emergency response and post-disaster monitoring.

1.3 Monitoring Applications

Studies of LiDAR in coastal areas have expanded to include:

  • Topographic mapping of beaches with millimetric precision
  • Assessment of vegetation canopies in coastal dune systems
  • Assessment of coral reefs using topobathymetric systems
  • Assessment of landslides and subsidence in coastal bluffs
  • Study of geomorphic changes caused by storms and sedimentation

Latest Development (2025)

A fusion system combining 3D LiDAR with video cameras, capable of simultaneously capturing 3D coordinates and surface colors of beaches and nearshore waves, was tested at two coastal sites in Japan. This system enables minutely monitoring of swash zone processes, demonstrating high potential for fine-scale coastal dynamics research.

2. LiDAR for Sea Turtle Nesting Habitat Monitoring

2.1 Padre Island Kemp's Ridley Study (2020)

Key Study: Figlus et al. (2020), "Using Lidar Data to Assess the Relationship Between Beach Geomorphology and Kemp's Ridley (Lepidochelys kempii) Nest Site Selection Along Padre Island, TX, United States." Frontiers in Marine Science, 7:214.

Major Findings for Kemp's Ridley at PAIS

  • Beach geomorphology characteristics (width and slope) were extracted from LiDAR-derived digital elevation models and successfully associated with Kemp's nest coordinates
  • Most important variables in nest presence models: elevation, distance from shoreline, maximum dune slope, and average beach slope
  • Study successfully modeled Kemp's ridley nest presence using beach geomorphology characteristics alone
  • Demonstrated that LiDAR-derived metrics can predict nesting habitat suitability without requiring biological surveys

This research provides the direct precedent for using MLS data to analyze Kemp's ridley habitat at PAIS. However, the 2020 study used single-date LiDAR data. The opportunity exists to build on this work with multi-temporal MLS datasets to examine temporal stability and change.

2.2 Mississippi Barrier Islands Assessment

A USGS project identified potential sea turtle nesting habitat on Cat and Ship Islands using digital elevation models developed from LiDAR data and dune toes from LiDAR-derived beach morphology. While focused on different species, this study demonstrates methodology for habitat potential mapping that could be adapted for Kemp's ridley.

2.3 Conservation Context for Kemp's Ridley

2023-2024 Nesting Activity at PAIS

  • More Kemp's ridley nests are found at PAIS than any other location in the U.S.
  • Record year (2017): 353 nests documented at PAIS
  • 2024 first nest: Mid-April discovery with 81 eggs
  • Species status: Critically Endangered (IUCN)
  • Recovery program ongoing since 1978
  • Primary threat: Bycatch in commercial and recreational fishing gear

3. Multi-Temporal LiDAR Erosion Mapping and Change Detection

3.1 DEMs of Difference (DoD) Methodology

The standard approach for multi-temporal LiDAR analysis involves computing DEMs of Difference (DoD), resulting in segregated maps of erosion and deposition and sediment budgets. To account for LiDAR dataset accuracy, a probabilistic approach at a 95% confidence interval is used as a threshold for Geomorphic Change Detection, showing more reliable results than simple differencing.

3.2 Volume Change Analysis

LiDAR-based digital elevation models are widely used for quantification of beach and dune volume change, particularly for studying storm impacts. Four LiDAR datasets from Ship Island, Mississippi (2004, 2007, 2010, 2012) capturing complete topography revealed erosional/depositional patterns and geomorphologic changes, investigating sediment volume changes at depths up to 8 m.

3.3 French Coast Multi-Temporal Study (5-Year Series)

Benchmark Study: Three beach and dune systems in the Bay of Biscay, France, monitored over 5 years with three airborne LiDAR datasets. Reproducible monitoring solutions adapted to basic and complex beach and dune morphologies were investigated.

Key Methodological Contributions

  • Developed shoreline mobility indicators combining horizontal and vertical migrations of the dune base
  • Created reproducible workflows for simple and complex beach morphologies
  • Demonstrated importance of multi-period analysis (not just two-date comparisons)
  • Established protocols for probabilistic change detection at 95% confidence

3.4 Advanced 4D Geospatial Analysis

Recent developments (2019-2024) in spatiotemporal segmentation enable 4D objects-by-change analysis from LiDAR time series. Geographic observation benefits from increasing availability of 3D geospatial data time series, allowing analysis of change processes at high temporal detail over extensive periods.

3.5 Machine Learning Integration

Machine Learning for Shoreline Change (2024-2025)

Machine learning techniques integrated with remote sensing and GIS improve predictive accuracy of shoreline change models, with Random Forest classifiers achieving ~90% accuracy for detecting coastal degradation zones when applied to LiDAR and orthophoto data. Algorithms analyze historical shoreline data to recognize patterns and generate future predictions.

4. Beach Morphology and Nesting Behavior Relationships

4.1 Beach Slope as a Selection Factor

Cross-Species Slope Preferences

Beach slope is a key environmental factor explaining nest site selection:

  • Green and Loggerhead turtles (Australia): Successful nests on beaches sloped between 3.5° and 6.7°
  • Low slope beaches: Smaller numbers of nests
  • Mid-level slope: High nest density (Brazil study)
  • Loggerheads: Slope has largest impact, correlated with nest elevation
  • Green turtles: Prefer steeper slopes and steeper berm slopes
  • Leatherbacks: Actively select sites not undergoing erosion; slope used as cue

4.2 Multi-Factor Habitat Assessment

A 2016 study demonstrated that marine turtles are "not fussy nesters" when examined at small spatial scales, but geomorphology (elevation, slope), temperature, humidity, hydrodynamics, and human activity all influence nesting suitability at larger scales. Specific nest placement in terms of distance from high water line, vegetation, slope, and elevation varies by species and location.

4.3 Future Sea Level Rise Impacts

SLR Impact Assessment (2020): Study quantified impacts of future sea level rise on nesting sea turtles in the southeastern United States. Analysis showed that beach morphology changes under SLR scenarios will significantly alter nesting habitat availability and suitability.

5. Emerging Methods and Advanced Applications

5.1 Terrain Roughness and Texture Analysis

LiDAR terrain data characterization of surface roughness and microtopography has advanced significantly. A 2019 method for estimating multiscale surface roughness based on standard deviation of surface normals utilizes scale partitioning and integral image processing. As fine-resolution LiDAR DEMs become increasingly common, multiscale analysis has potential for improving mapping and classification applications.

Coastal Habitat Classification (2022)

Topobathymetric LiDAR relevance for coastal and estuarine habitat mapping demonstrated by classifying bispectral data to produce 3D maps of 21 land and sea covers at very high resolution. Research showed possibilities for exploiting spatial information through addition of geometrical features such as roughness or local density.

5.2 Dune Toe Migration Detection

A Toe Line Tracking (TLT) method enables automated measurement of dune migration rate and direction using multi-temporal DEMs. The method automatically extracts the base level of a dune field and toe lines of individual dunes based on simple parameters.

Technical Implementation:

  • Multi-temporal LiDAR point clouds processed using IDW interpolation (1×1 m cell size)
  • Toe line polygons derived from two DEMs processed using logical operators and spatial analysis
  • Thousands of random sampling points generated along source toe lines to calculate migration
  • Demonstrated on 9 km × 2.4 km area with three time periods

5.3 Habitat Suitability Modeling with LiDAR Metrics

LiDAR metrics can be harmonized into five morphological traits: canopy height, canopy cover, canopy vertical distribution, understory/shrubland, and terrain topography. Studies demonstrate valuable contribution of LiDAR altimetry and topographically-derived features in coastal habitat mapping, with synergy between LiDAR and visible/near-infrared bands enabling discrimination of specific communities.

5.4 Sea Level Rise Vulnerability Assessment

LiDAR-Enhanced SLR Vulnerability (2023)

New LiDAR-based elevation models show greatest increase in global coastal exposure to flooding from early-stage SLR. CoastalDEM utilizing neural networks to reduce SRTM error shows 190 million people currently occupy global land below projected high tide lines for 2100, tripling SRTM-based values.

LiDAR-derived elevation data provide significantly better spatial resolution and vertical accuracy (25 cm at 95% confidence) for delineating lands vulnerable to sea level rise, making them much more appropriate than previous DEMs for meter-range SLR projections.

5.5 Beach Microtopography Analysis

Terrestrial laser scanning (TLS) provides highly detailed microtopography with millimetric measurement precision. High-resolution 1 cm microtopographic surface models demonstrate RMSE = 3.67 ± 1 cm precision and bias = 1.26 ± 0.1 cm accuracy across study sites. This level of detail enables characterization of fine-scale beach surface variability potentially relevant to sea turtle nesting substrate quality.

5.6 Beach Vulnerability Indices

Beach Vulnerability Index (BVI): Newer vulnerability assessment approach combining simplicity in calculations, easily obtainable data, and low processing capacity. Provides results not only for different beaches but also for different sectors of the same beach.

Recent frameworks integrate Bayesian Networks with modern coastal vulnerability frameworks, achieving >90% accuracy in correlating beach types with vulnerability factors, significantly outperforming traditional Coastal Vulnerability Index (CVI) applications.

5.7 CloudCompare and LAStools Workflows

Practical workflows for coastal DEM analysis using CloudCompare and LAStools have been documented:

  • LASnoise: Classify noise points into class 7
  • las2dem: Generate raster with heights while excluding noise class
  • CloudCompare: Create contour lines, DEM, DTM, DSM from point clouds
  • Coastal applications: Shoreline change mapping using citizen science approaches

6. Synthesis and Research Context

6.1 Padre Island Monitoring Programs

The Gulf Coast Network (GULN) conducts geomorphology monitoring at PAIS every other year as part of the NPS Vital Signs Monitoring Program. Beach width is defined using LiDAR DEMs (e.g., 2018 LiDAR DEM). Since the 2021 survey, there has been overall seaward advance in shoreline position accompanied by increase in dune profile area.

Long-term Shoreline Change Trends:

  • South Padre Island and Brazos Island: Average retreat rate 2.2 m/yr (1937-2013)
  • Recent period (2000-2013): Decreased to 1.1 m/yr retreat
  • PAIS (since 2021): Seaward advance and dune area increase

6.2 Predictive Modeling Advances

Computer models now predict shoreline change due to waves and sea-level rise, with projections estimating 24-75% of California beaches may become completely eroded by 2100 under different SLR scenarios. ShoreShop2.0, a blind benchmarking workshop, stress-tested current generation shoreline models with 34 teams worldwide.

6.3 Climate Change Adaptation Framework

Coastal regions face increasing threats from climate change-induced hazards, necessitating accurate and comprehensive vulnerability assessment tools. Integration of machine learning with multi-dimensional datasets collected over extended periods offers advantages for filling data gaps, predicting in data-scarce regions, and analyzing long-term trends.

Critical Research Needs Identified

  • Multi-temporal Mobile LiDAR applications for habitat monitoring remain limited
  • Fine-scale morphological variability within nesting beaches poorly characterized
  • Temporal stability of habitat characteristics largely unknown
  • Integration of SLR projections with species-specific habitat requirements underdeveloped
  • Predictive habitat models based on morphodynamic change patterns needed
  • Management-ready vulnerability metrics and decision tools lacking

6.4 Implications for Thesis Research

This literature review demonstrates that while Mobile LiDAR technology is proven for coastal monitoring and habitat characterization, several critical opportunities exist for novel research using the PAIS 2024-2026 MLS datasets:

  1. Multi-temporal perspective: Most studies use single-date or limited temporal data; three-year MLS time series enables new temporal analyses
  2. Fine-scale focus: MLS provides detail (cm-level) exceeding most published coastal LiDAR studies
  3. Species-specific application: Kemp's ridley focus for critically endangered species at primary nesting site
  4. Predictive capacity: Integration of change detection with future habitat projection largely unexplored
  5. Management orientation: Opportunity to develop practical tools for PAIS conservation decisions
