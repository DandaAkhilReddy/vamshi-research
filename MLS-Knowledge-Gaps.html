Introduction

Based on the comprehensive literature review (2018-2025), eight critical knowledge gaps have been identified that your MLS thesis can address. These gaps represent areas where the 2024-2026 PAIS Mobile LiDAR dataset can make novel contributions to coastal science and conservation management, particularly for Kemp's ridley sea turtle habitat monitoring.

1 Temporal Stability of Nesting Habitat Characteristics

Current Knowledge Limitation

The 2020 Figlus et al. study at PAIS successfully linked beach geomorphology to Kemp's ridley nest site selection using LiDAR-derived metrics (elevation, beach slope, dune slope, distance from shoreline). However, this analysis used single-date LiDAR data. No studies have examined how temporal variability in these geomorphic characteristics affects habitat suitability or nest site persistence over multiple years.

Research Opportunity

Your 3-year MLS time series (2024, 2025, 2026) enables assessment of:

  • Which beach segments maintain stable geomorphic characteristics favorable for nesting
  • How slope, elevation, and beach width variability correlates with nest site reuse patterns
  • Temporal persistence vs. ephemerality of optimal nesting zones
  • Seasonal and annual morphodynamic changes affecting habitat quality

Potential Metrics to Develop:

  • Morphological Stability Index (MSI): Quantifies consistency of key habitat parameters over time
  • Habitat Persistence Mapping: Identifies zones maintaining suitability thresholds across all surveys
  • Change Magnitude Classification: Low/moderate/high change zones relative to nesting requirements

2 Fine-Scale Microtopographic Variability and Nesting Substrate Quality

Current Knowledge Limitation

Existing sea turtle habitat studies focus on macro-scale parameters (beach width, slope, elevation). Literature on wetland and agricultural microtopography demonstrates LiDAR can capture millimetric to centimetric surface variability, but this has not been applied to sea turtle nesting beaches. Surface roughness, texture, and micro-elevation patterns may influence:

  • Substrate compaction and digging effort required
  • Micro-drainage patterns affecting egg chamber moisture
  • Surface stability during nest construction

Research Opportunity

Mobile LiDAR's high point density (~700,000 pts/sec) at cm-level accuracy enables novel analysis of:

  • Surface roughness metrics (standard deviation of surface normals, terrain ruggedness index)
  • Micro-slope variability within nesting zones vs. non-nesting zones
  • Texture analysis using moving window statistics
  • Micro-drainage patterns from high-resolution DEMs

Feasibility with MLS Data:

CloudCompare enables calculation of roughness and surface normal variation. ArcGIS Pro's Spatial Analyst provides terrain ruggedness, curvature, and focal statistics tools. No coding required.

3 Dune-Beach System Vulnerability at Habitat Scale

Current Knowledge Limitation

Beach Vulnerability Index (BVI) and Coastal Vulnerability Index (CVI) approaches assess erosion risk and SLR susceptibility, but these frameworks are not tailored to wildlife habitat resilience. They lack integration of:

  • Species-specific habitat requirements
  • Habitat availability thresholds
  • Recovery potential of suitable nesting zones post-disturbance

General vulnerability assessments don't address whether "vulnerable" beaches still provide functional habitat or how long habitat suitability persists under change.

Research Opportunity

Develop a Habitat-Specific Vulnerability Index for Kemp's ridley nesting beaches integrating:

  • Traditional geomorphic vulnerability parameters (slope, erosion rate, beach width change)
  • Kemp's ridley habitat suitability thresholds from Figlus et al. (2020)
  • Temporal stability metrics from multi-year MLS
  • Dune toe migration and protective capacity
  • NOAA SLR projections for PAIS

Management Value:

Maps showing "vulnerable habitat zones" vs. "resilient habitat zones" provide actionable information for PAIS prioritizing conservation interventions, beach nourishment decisions, and nest relocation policies.

4 Predictive Habitat Availability Under Sea-Level Rise Scenarios

Current Knowledge Limitation

Studies quantifying SLR impacts on sea turtle nesting (e.g., 2020 study on southeastern U.S.) use static inundation modeling—simply overlaying future sea levels on current topography. This approach ignores:

  • Active morphodynamic processes (erosion, accretion, dune migration)
  • Beach response to SLR varying by location
  • Habitat suitability changes beyond simple inundation

No studies use multi-temporal LiDAR change rates to project future beach morphology and then assess habitat under combined morphodynamic + SLR scenarios.

Research Opportunity

Use 2024-2026 MLS change detection to derive annual change rates, then:

  1. Extrapolate observed erosion/accretion trends to 2030, 2040, 2050
  2. Integrate NOAA SLR intermediate and high scenarios
  3. Model future DEMs combining morphodynamic change + SLR
  4. Apply Kemp's ridley suitability criteria to future beach configurations
  5. Quantify habitat loss, shift, and potential gain areas

Workflow (ArcGIS Pro - No Coding):

  • Raster Calculator: DEM₂₀₂₆ - DEM₂₀₂₄ = change over 2 years
  • Derive annual change rate raster
  • Multiply by projection years and add to 2026 DEM
  • Add SLR scenarios to shoreline elevation
  • Apply suitability thresholds as conditional rasters

5 Automated Dune Toe Detection and Migration for Habitat Protection Assessment

Current Knowledge Limitation

Dune toe position is critical for defining landward extent of nesting beach and protective buffering from overwash. While Toe Line Tracking (TLT) methods exist for desert dunes, coastal applications are limited. Most coastal studies manually digitize dune toes—time-consuming and subjective.

No studies have linked dune toe migration patterns to changes in available nesting habitat area or habitat quality metrics.

Research Opportunity

Develop semi-automated dune toe extraction workflow using CloudCompare/ArcGIS Pro:

  • Slope threshold method: Identify dune toe where slope exceeds threshold (e.g., 15°-20°)
  • Profile-based extraction: Cross-shore transects identify inflection point at dune base
  • Time-series analysis: Quantify dune toe migration 2024-2026
  • Habitat impact assessment: Correlate dune toe retreat with loss of back-beach nesting zones

Management Application:

Map zones where dune toe erosion threatens to eliminate high-suitability nesting habitat. Inform dune restoration and beach nourishment prioritization.

6 Beach Morphodynamic Classification for Habitat Forecasting

Current Knowledge Limitation

Beaches exhibit different morphodynamic behaviors: some are erosional, some accreting, some stable, some highly dynamic. Literature on machine learning for coastal change (achieving 90% accuracy) focuses on predicting where change occurs, not how different behavior types affect habitat persistence.

Research Opportunity

Classify PAIS beach segments into morphodynamic behavior types using multi-temporal MLS:

  • Type 1 - Stable: Minimal change in elevation, slope, beach width
  • Type 2 - Accreting: Increasing elevation and/or width
  • Type 3 - Eroding: Decreasing elevation and/or width
  • Type 4 - Dynamic: High variability but no directional trend
  • Type 5 - Critical: Rapid erosion threatening habitat thresholds

Then overlay with nest locations to determine which behavior types support successful nesting.

Feasibility (No Coding):

ArcGIS Pro's Image Classification tools or manual classification based on DoD statistics within segments. Spatial Analyst's Zonal Statistics calculates change metrics per segment.

7 Multi-Scale Habitat Assessment Framework

Current Knowledge Limitation

Habitat suitability studies analyze features at single spatial scales (e.g., 1-m resolution DEMs). However, geomorphic processes and biological responses operate across multiple scales:

  • Micro-scale (cm): Surface texture, substrate quality
  • Meso-scale (m to 10s of m): Slope, elevation, beach width
  • Macro-scale (100s of m to km): Beach segment behavior, regional patterns

No studies integrate suitability assessments across these nested scales.

Research Opportunity

Develop hierarchical habitat suitability framework:

  1. Macro-scale: Segment-level morphodynamic classification
  2. Meso-scale: Slope, elevation, beach width suitability (Figlus et al. criteria)
  3. Micro-scale: Surface roughness and texture quality
  4. Integration: Composite suitability score combining all scales

8 Operational Monitoring Framework for Adaptive Management

Current Knowledge Limitation

Most LiDAR coastal studies are research-focused, producing scientific publications but not operational tools for park managers. PAIS staff need:

  • Simple, interpretable habitat quality maps
  • Change detection alerts for critical areas
  • Decision support for nest relocation priorities
  • Repeatable workflows for future MLS surveys (2027, 2028, etc.)

Gap exists between sophisticated geospatial analysis and management-ready tools.

Research Opportunity

Create management-oriented monitoring framework deliverables:

  • Standardized habitat quality maps with color-coded zones (excellent/good/fair/poor/unsuitable)
  • Change alert system: Flags for areas exceeding critical erosion thresholds
  • Priority matrices: Rankings of beach segments for intervention
  • Workflow documentation: Step-by-step ArcGIS Pro procedures for future surveys
  • Dashboard-style summary reports: Key metrics tracking over time
