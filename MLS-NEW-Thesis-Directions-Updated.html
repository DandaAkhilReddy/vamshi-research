🆕 NEW Direction 1: Seasonal Morphological Stability Index for Nesting-to-Hatching Habitat Persistence

Research Question

"Which beach segments at PAIS maintain stable, suitable morphology from the nesting season (May-June) through the hatching season (August-September), and can a Seasonal Persistence Index identify zones where nests laid in May remain viable through August emergence?"

How This Differs from RA Project

RA Project Focus Your Thesis Focus
Overall erosion/change rates between surveys Seasonal stability within each nesting cycle (May → August)
Long-term SLR vulnerability (10/25/50 years) Short-term seasonal habitat persistence (2-3 month windows)
"Where will habitat be lost by 2060?" "Which May nests are threatened by August erosion?"
Site-level change quantification Nest-scale habitat persistence mapping

Unique Advantage of Twice-Yearly Surveys

Your RA's May-June and Aug-Sept timing is perfectly aligned with Kemp's ridley biology:

  • May-June: Peak nesting season → eggs deposited
  • 45-70 day incubation: Nests vulnerable to erosion, inundation, thermal stress
  • August-September: Peak hatching → emergence success depends on nest not being eroded

Management Value: Identifies beach segments where May nests have high probability of August emergence success vs. segments where erosion threatens nest viability mid-season.

Datasets and Metrics

Primary Data (From Your RA):

  • May DEMs: 2024, 2025, 2026 (3 nesting season baselines)
  • August DEMs: 2024, 2025, 2026 (3 hatching season endpoints)
  • Nest GPS data: Dr. Shaver's database (May-June nest locations)
  • Hatch success records: If available (% emerged per nest)

Derived Metrics (New for Thesis):

Metric Calculation Interpretation
Seasonal Elevation Change (SEC) DEM_Aug - DEM_May (per pixel) Erosion (-) or accretion (+) during incubation period
Nest Zone Stability (NZS) Std Dev of SEC within 50m radius of each nest Low variability = stable nest environment
Shoreline Migration Rate (SMR) (Aug shoreline position - May shoreline) / days m/day landward retreat during season
Nest Inundation Risk (NIR) If nest elevation < Aug storm tide level Binary: threatened or safe
Seasonal Persistence Index (SPI) Composite of SEC, NZS, SMR, NIR 0-100 score; higher = more persistent habitat

Complete Workflow (ArcGIS Pro)

Phase 1: Seasonal Change Detection (Per Year)

  1. For 2024:
    • DEM_Aug2024 - DEM_May2024 = SEC_2024
    • Extract May2024 and Aug2024 shorelines (mean high water contour)
    • Calculate shoreline displacement (DSAS or manual transects)
  2. Repeat for 2025 and 2026 → SEC_2025, SEC_2026
  3. Average seasonal change: (SEC_2024 + SEC_2025 + SEC_2026) / 3 = Mean SEC

Phase 2: Nest-Specific Analysis

  1. Import nest points (May GPS locations from Dr. Shaver)
  2. Extract to Points: SEC values at each nest location
  3. Buffer Analysis: Create 50m buffer around each nest
    • Zonal Statistics: Mean, Std Dev, Min, Max of SEC within buffer
    • NZS = Std Dev (variability around nest)
  4. Shoreline Distance: Near tool (nest points to Aug shoreline)
    • If distance < 10m → High erosion threat
    • If distance > 50m → Safe from seasonal erosion

Phase 3: Seasonal Persistence Index Calculation

  1. Normalize metrics to 0-1 scale:
    • Low erosion = high score
    • Low variability = high score
    • Far from shoreline = high score
  2. Weighted sum: SPI = (40% × SEC_norm) + (30% × NZS_norm) + (30% × Distance_norm)
  3. Classify:
    • SPI > 80: Very Persistent (nest likely safe)
    • SPI 60-80: Persistent (moderate risk)
    • SPI 40-60: Moderately Unstable
    • SPI 20-40: Unstable (high erosion risk)
    • SPI < 20: Critical (nest likely compromised)

Phase 4: Validation with Hatch Success (If Data Available)

  1. Obtain hatch success % for each nest from Dr. Shaver
  2. Join success data to nest points attribute table
  3. Statistical test: Correlation between SPI and hatch success
    • Hypothesis: Nests in high-SPI zones have higher emergence rates
    • Scatter plot: SPI (x-axis) vs. % emerged (y-axis)
    • Linear regression: R² value indicates predictive power

Expected Outputs

Maps (12 total):

  • Seasonal Elevation Change maps: May→Aug for 2024, 2025, 2026 (3 maps)
  • Seasonal Persistence Index map (composite)
  • Nest location overlay with SPI zones
  • High-Risk Nest Zones (SPI < 40) requiring relocation consideration
  • Optimal Nest Zones (SPI > 80) for protection priority
  • Shoreline migration during incubation period (3 years)
  • Site-specific SPI maps for each of 3-4 RA sites

Quantitative Results:

  • Table: % of nests in each SPI class per year
  • Mean seasonal erosion rate (cm/day during May-Aug)
  • Number of nests threatened by shoreline advance (< 10m buffer)
  • Correlation coefficient: SPI vs. hatch success (if data available)

Management Deliverables for Dr. Shaver:

  • Nest Relocation Priority List: Rank nests by SPI; recommend relocating < 40
  • Beach Segment Rankings: Identify 100m segments with consistently high SPI → focus monitoring
  • Early Warning Thresholds: If May-to-June change exceeds X cm → predict high Aug risk

How 2026 Data Improves Analysis

  • Completes 3-year validation of SPI (are high-SPI zones consistently safe?)
  • Captures inter-annual variability (2024 may be anomalous; 2025-2026 shows pattern)
  • Enables trend analysis: Is seasonal erosion accelerating?
  • Provides 2026 nest data to test predictive accuracy of SPI model

Limitations and Considerations

  • No storm modeling: Mid-season hurricanes can cause abrupt change not captured by May/Aug snapshots
  • Hatch success confounders: Predation, temperature, inundation all affect emergence (not just erosion)
  • Assumes linear change: May→Aug change rate may not be constant throughout incubation
  • Mitigation: Focus interpretation on morphological persistence, acknowledge other biological factors

Publication Potential

  • Endangered Species Research - links geomorphology to hatch success
  • Frontiers in Marine Science - seasonal habitat dynamics for sea turtles
  • Chelonian Conservation and Biology - management-oriented conservation tool
  • Estuarine, Coastal and Shelf Science - seasonal morphodynamics

🆕 NEW Direction 2: Vehicle Access Impact on Beach Morphological Resilience and Habitat Quality

Research Question

"Do vehicle-access and no-vehicle-access beach segments at PAIS exhibit different morphological stability, erosion rates, and habitat suitability patterns, and should conservation strategies prioritize vehicle-free zones for Kemp's ridley nesting protection?"

How This Differs from RA Project

RA Project Focus Your Thesis Focus
Site selection based on access patterns Comparative analysis of access vs. no-access impacts
SLR vulnerability assessment (natural processes) Anthropogenic disturbance assessment (vehicle impacts)
Treats access as site characteristic Tests access as predictor variable for habitat quality

Unique Opportunity from Site Selection Protocol

Your RA project intentionally includes both vehicle-access and no-access sites. This creates a natural experiment to test:

  • Does vehicle compaction alter beach morphology?
  • Are vehicle-free zones more morphologically stable?
  • Do no-access areas maintain higher habitat suitability over time?

Management Value: Evidence-based recommendation on expanding vehicle-free zones for habitat protection.

Methodology

Phase 1: Site Classification

  • Classify each 4.5km site as: Vehicle-Access (VA) or No-Vehicle-Access (NVA)
  • Within VA sites, delineate vehicle corridors (from tire tracks visible in orthoimagery or MLS intensity)
  • Within NVA sites, confirm no vehicle tracks

Phase 2: Comparative Morphometric Analysis

Metric VA Sites NVA Sites Statistical Test
Erosion Rate Mean SEC (May→Aug) Mean SEC (May→Aug) Independent t-test
Elevation Variability CV across 2024-2026 CV across 2024-2026 Mann-Whitney U
Beach Slope Mean slope Mean slope t-test
Surface Roughness TRI from MLS TRI from MLS t-test
Nest Density Nests/km Nests/km Chi-square

Phase 3: Within-Site Analysis (Vehicle Corridors vs. Undisturbed Zones)

For VA sites only:

  • Delineate 10m-wide vehicle corridor polygons
  • Compare metrics in corridor vs. 50m+ away from corridor
  • Test: Does proximity to vehicle traffic correlate with morphological change?

Expected Outcomes

Potential Findings (Hypotheses to Test):

  • Hypothesis 1: NVA sites show lower erosion rates (less compaction = more stable)
  • Hypothesis 2: NVA sites have higher nest density (turtles prefer undisturbed zones)
  • Hypothesis 3: VA sites show more surface roughness variability (tire ruts)
  • Hypothesis 4: Within VA sites, nests avoid vehicle corridors

Management Recommendations Based on Results:

  • If NVA superior: Recommend expanding vehicle-free zones
  • If VA similar: Current access policy not harmful
  • If VA corridors avoided by nests: Recommend seasonal closures during nesting

Workflow Summary

  1. Classify sites (VA vs. NVA) and segments (corridor vs. undisturbed)
  2. Calculate morphometric metrics for each category
  3. Statistical comparison using t-tests, ANOVA, chi-square
  4. Spatial analysis of nest distribution relative to vehicle corridors
  5. Generate comparative maps and tables

Publication Potential

  • Ocean & Coastal Management - human impact assessment
  • Coastal Management - vehicle access policy evaluation
  • Marine Turtle Newsletter - practical management findings

🆕 NEW Direction 3: PAIS-Specific Habitat Persistence Thresholds Under Incremental SLR (0.4m, 0.8m, 1.34m)

Research Question

"At what specific SLR increments (0.4m, 0.8m, 1.34m) do critical habitat suitability thresholds fail at PAIS, and which beach segments will transition from 'optimal' to 'unsuitable' at each SLR milestone based on observed 2024-2026 morphodynamic baselines?"

How This Differs from RA Project

RA Project Focus Your Thesis Focus
General SLR vulnerability modeling (10/25/50 yr projections) Habitat-specific threshold exceedance analysis at PAIS SLR increments
"How much habitat will be lost?" "When/where do specific habitat requirements fail?"
Future scenarios (2034, 2049, 2074) Threshold timing analysis (when does 0.4m occur? 0.8m? 1.34m?)

Leveraging PAIS-Specific SLR Data

Your RA uses NOAA/USACE projections specific to PAIS: 0.4m to 1.34m by 2060 (36% above global average). This allows threshold-based analysis:

  • Low SLR (0.4m): ~2040-2045 under intermediate scenario
  • Mid SLR (0.8m): ~2050 under high scenario
  • High SLR (1.34m): ~2060 upper bound

Methodology

Phase 1: Baseline Habitat Suitability (2024-2026 Mean)

  • Define Kemp's ridley requirements (from Figlus et al. 2020):
    • Elevation: 1.5-4.0m above MHW
    • Slope: 3.5-6.7°
    • Beach width: > 30m
    • Distance from shoreline: 10-100m
  • Map current (2024-2026 average) suitable zones
  • Calculate baseline suitable area (ha)

Phase 2: Incremental SLR Application

  1. +0.4m SLR:
    • Raise MHW by 0.4m
    • Recalculate: elevation thresholds now 1.9-4.4m
    • New shoreline position (bathtub model)
    • Recalculate beach width (reduced)
    • Map new suitable zones
  2. +0.8m SLR: Repeat with 2.3-4.8m elevation threshold
  3. +1.34m SLR: Repeat with 2.84-5.34m elevation threshold

Phase 3: Threshold Exceedance Mapping

  • Identify which criterion fails first at each location:
    • Inundation (elevation below threshold)?
    • Beach too narrow (< 30m)?
    • Slope changed due to profile adjustment?
  • Create "failure mode" map showing why/where habitat is lost

Expected Outputs

Tables:

  • Suitable area (ha) at Baseline, +0.4m, +0.8m, +1.34m
  • % habitat loss at each increment
  • Habitat loss rate (ha per 0.1m SLR)
  • Breakdown: % lost to inundation vs. width reduction vs. other

Maps:

  • Baseline suitable habitat
  • Suitable habitat at +0.4m, +0.8m, +1.34m (4 maps total)
  • Threshold exceedance map (color-coded by failure mode)
  • Resilient zones (suitable even at +1.34m)

Timeline Analysis:

  • If NOAA intermediate scenario: 0.4m by ~2045 → X% habitat lost
  • If high scenario: 0.8m by ~2050 → Y% habitat lost
  • Critical threshold: At what SLR increment does > 50% habitat disappear?

Management Value

  • Identifies time-sensitive decision points (e.g., "By 2045 we'll lose segments 5-12")
  • Prioritizes zones resilient to +1.34m for long-term protection
  • Informs beach nourishment targets (e.g., "Add 2m elevation to segment X to delay loss")

How This Uses RA Data Uniquely

  • RA provides baseline DEMs and morphodynamic rates
  • Thesis applies PAIS-specific SLR increments systematically
  • RA focuses on "what" changes; thesis focuses on "when critical thresholds exceeded"

Publication Potential

  • Global Change Biology - threshold-based habitat loss
  • Climatic Change - incremental SLR impacts
  • Endangered Species Research - time-sensitive conservation planning
