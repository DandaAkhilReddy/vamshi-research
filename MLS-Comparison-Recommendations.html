Direction 4: Predictive Nesting Habitat Availability Under Sea-Level Rise Scenarios

Research Question:

How will suitable Kemp's ridley nesting habitat availability change at PAIS by 2030, 2040, and 2050 under NOAA intermediate and high SLR scenarios when accounting for both sea-level rise AND observed morphodynamic change rates (erosion, accretion, dune migration)?

Novelty & Differentiation from RA Project

RA Project Focus: Assessing current SLR vulnerability using static inundation modeling

Thesis Focus: Dynamic predictive modeling integrating morphodynamic change rates with SLR to forecast future beach configurations and habitat suitability

Novel Contribution: First study to combine multi-temporal LiDAR-derived morphodynamic rates with SLR projections to model future turtle nesting habitat (vs. simple inundation mapping)

Datasets & Metrics

  • MLS DEMs: 2024, 2025, 2026 (1m resolution)
  • Shoreline positions: Extracted from each DEM
  • Beach width transects: Every 25m along shore
  • Elevation change rasters: DoD (2024-2025, 2025-2026)
  • NOAA SLR Projections: Intermediate (+0.5m by 2050) and High (+1.0m by 2050) scenarios for Texas coast
  • Habitat suitability thresholds: From Figlus et al. (2020) - elevation, slope, distance from water, beach width

Key Derived Metrics:

  • Annual Erosion/Accretion Rate (m/yr): Linear regression of elevation change
  • Shoreline Change Rate (m/yr): DSAS End Point Rate or Linear Regression Rate
  • Beach Width Change Rate (m/yr): Transect-based width trend
  • Future DEMs: Projected for 2030, 2040, 2050
  • Habitat Area: Hectares of suitable nesting beach for each time period
  • Habitat Loss Rate: % loss per decade

Complete Workflow

Phase 1: Calculate Morphodynamic Change Rates

  1. Elevation Change Rate: (DEM_2026 - DEM_2024) / 2 years = m/yr per pixel
  2. Shoreline Migration Rate: DSAS tool with 2024, 2025, 2026 shorelines → LRR (m/yr) per transect
  3. Beach Width Trend: Linear regression of width measurements across 3 years per transect

Phase 2: Project Future Beach Morphology

  1. 2030 DEM: DEM_2026 + (ElevationChangeRate × 4 years)
  2. 2040 DEM: DEM_2026 + (ElevationChangeRate × 14 years)
  3. 2050 DEM: DEM_2026 + (ElevationChangeRate × 24 years)
  4. Use Raster Calculator for pixel-by-pixel addition

Phase 3: Apply SLR Scenarios

  1. Intermediate Scenario: Mean High Water +0.3m (2030), +0.4m (2040), +0.5m (2050)
  2. High Scenario: MHW +0.5m (2030), +0.75m (2040), +1.0m (2050)
  3. Create new waterline: Con tool (Elevation < MHW + SLR = "Inundated", Elevation ≥ MHW + SLR = "Terrestrial")
  4. Extract new shoreline positions from inundation boundary

Phase 4: Adjust Beach Width for Combined Effects

  1. New shore position moves landward due to SLR
  2. Dune position also migrates based on dune toe retreat rate (from Direction 2/3)
  3. Recalculate beach width = distance between new shoreline and adjusted dune toe
  4. Result: Future beach width accounting for both SLR inundation AND morphodynamic change

Phase 5: Apply Habitat Suitability Criteria

Kemp's Ridley Requirements (Figlus et al. 2020):

  • Elevation: 1.5-4.0 m above MHW (adjust for SLR)
  • Slope: 3.5-6.7°
  • Beach Width: > 30 m (minimum)
  • Distance from Shoreline: 10-100 m

Raster Calculator Suitability Model:

Suitable = (Elevation >= 1.5 & Elevation <= 4.0) & (Slope >= 3.5 & Slope <= 6.7) & (BeachWidth > 30) & (DistToShore >= 10 & DistToShore <= 100)

Phase 6: Calculate Habitat Area Changes

  1. Baseline (2026): Area of suitable habitat (ha)
  2. 2030: Suitable area under both SLR scenarios
  3. 2040: Suitable area under both scenarios
  4. 2050: Suitable area under both scenarios
  5. Calculate % change, absolute loss (ha), loss rate (%/decade)

Phase 7: Spatial Analysis of Habitat Shifts

  • Lost Habitat Zones: Suitable in 2026 but unsuitable in future → identify which criterion failed (inundation? width loss? slope change?)
  • Persistent Habitat Zones: Suitable in ALL time periods → conservation priorities
  • Emerging Habitat Zones: Unsuitable in 2026 but suitable in future (due to accretion/dune building) → unexpected opportunities

Expected Outputs

Maps (12 total):

  • Projected DEMs for 2030, 2040, 2050
  • Habitat suitability maps for 2026 (baseline), 2030, 2040, 2050 under both SLR scenarios (6 maps)
  • Habitat change maps: Lost vs. Persistent vs. Emerging zones
  • Spatial vulnerability map: where/why habitat is lost (inundation, width loss, slope change)

Quantitative Results:

  • Table: Habitat area (ha) for baseline and all future scenarios
  • % habitat loss by 2030, 2040, 2050 (Intermediate vs. High SLR)
  • Linear loss rate (ha/year, %/decade)
  • Breakdown: % lost due to inundation vs. width reduction vs. other factors

Management Deliverables:

  • Priority conservation zones (persistent habitat under all scenarios)
  • At-risk zones requiring intervention within 5, 10, 20 years
  • Recommendations for beach nourishment volumes/locations to offset projected losses

2026 MLS Data Contribution

Completes 3-year time series essential for calculating reliable annual change rates. Two-year rates (2024-2025) alone may be anomalous; 2026 provides validation that trends are consistent.

Limitations & Assumptions

  • Linear extrapolation assumption: Assumes 2024-2026 change rates continue at constant pace (may accelerate or decelerate)
  • No storm events modeled: Major hurricanes could cause abrupt changes not captured by linear trends
  • 3-year baseline short: Ideally 5-10 years for robust rate calculation
  • Uncertainty compounds over time: 2030 projections more reliable than 2050
  • Mitigation: Run sensitivity analysis with ±50% rate variations; acknowledge uncertainty in discussion; present as "scenarios" not "predictions"

Publication Venues

  • Ecological Applications - conservation forecasting
  • Global Change Biology - climate change impacts on biodiversity
  • Endangered Species Research - threatened species management
  • Estuarine, Coastal and Shelf Science - coastal habitat modeling

Direction 5: Fine-Scale Beach Morphodynamic Change Classification for Conservation Prioritization

Research Question:

Can PAIS nesting beaches be classified into distinct morphodynamic behavior types (stable, eroding, accreting, dynamic) based on multi-temporal MLS data, and do different behavior classes support different levels of nesting activity or success?

Novelty & Differentiation

RA Project: Assesses overall SLR vulnerability trends

Thesis: Creates behavioral classification system for beach segments to identify heterogeneous responses and inform adaptive management

Novel Contribution: First morphodynamic classification specifically designed for wildlife habitat management (vs. coastal engineering or hazard assessment)

Classification System

Class Characteristics Management Implication
Type 1: Stable Minimal elevation change (< ±0.1 m), low shoreline migration (< 0.5 m/yr), consistent beach width Focus intensive monitoring; reliable long-term habitat
Type 2: Accreting Positive elevation trend (> +0.1 m/yr), shoreline advancing, increasing width Improving habitat; may become optimal in future
Type 3: Eroding Negative elevation trend (< -0.1 m/yr), retreating shoreline, decreasing width Declining habitat; consider nourishment or restoration
Type 4: Dynamic (Cyclic) High variability but no trend (alternating erosion/accretion), CV > 50% Unpredictable; requires adaptive nest monitoring
Type 5: Critical Erosion Severe erosion (< -0.3 m/yr), rapid width loss, approaching habitat threshold failure Urgent intervention needed; may lose habitat within 5 years

Workflow

Step 1: Segment Beach into Analysis Units

  • Divide PAIS nesting zone into 100m alongshore segments (or use existing NPS monitoring segments)
  • Create segment polygons extending from shoreline to dune crest
  • Result: ~500-700 segments for entire study area

Step 2: Calculate Change Statistics Per Segment

Use Zonal Statistics tool for each segment polygon:

  • Mean elevation change 2024-2026: Average of DoD
  • Elevation trend: Linear regression if 3 time points (2024, 2025, 2026)
  • Elevation variability: Coefficient of Variation (CV) across 3 years
  • Shoreline change rate: From DSAS transects within segment
  • Beach width change: Mean width trend for transects in segment
  • Volume change: DoD × segment area = m³ erosion or accretion

Step 3: Apply Classification Criteria

Decision tree in segment attribute table:

IF Mean_Elev_Change > -0.05 AND Mean_Elev_Change < 0.05 AND CV_Elevation < 30%:
    Class = "Type 1: Stable"

ELIF Mean_Elev_Change > 0.1 AND Shoreline_Rate > 0:
    Class = "Type 2: Accreting"

ELIF Mean_Elev_Change < -0.1 AND Mean_Elev_Change > -0.3:
    Class = "Type 3: Eroding"

ELIF Mean_Elev_Change < -0.3 OR Beach_Width_Loss > 2 m/yr:
    Class = "Type 5: Critical Erosion"

ELSE:
    Class = "Type 4: Dynamic"

Implement using Field Calculator or Excel, then join back to segment shapefile

Step 4: Map Classification Results

  • Symbology: Color-code segments by type
  • Green = Stable, Blue = Accreting, Yellow = Eroding, Orange = Dynamic, Red = Critical
  • Calculate area (ha) and % of total nesting zone per class

Step 5: Overlay with Nest Data

  • Spatial Join: Nest points to segment polygons
  • Count nests per segment
  • Calculate nest density (nests/ha) per morphodynamic class
  • Statistical test: ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis → Is nest density significantly different among classes?

Step 6: Develop Management Priority Matrix

Morphodynamic Class High Nest Density Low Nest Density
Stable Priority 1: Maintain & Protect Priority 3: Monitor
Accreting Priority 2: Enhance Access Priority 4: Low
Eroding Priority 1: Urgent Intervention Priority 3: Assess if recoverable
Critical Erosion Priority 1: Emergency Action Priority 2: Stabilize or Abandon

Expected Outputs

  • Morphodynamic classification map (5 types, color-coded)
  • Summary statistics table: area and % per class
  • Nest density by class (bar chart, statistical test results)
  • Management priority map (4-tier system)
  • Segment-level database with all metrics for future analysis

2026 Contribution

Completes time series for trend analysis; validates classification (do "stable" segments remain stable in 2026?)

Limitations

  • Classification thresholds somewhat arbitrary (can run sensitivity analysis)
  • 3-year window captures only recent behavior (long-term cycles may differ)
  • Segment size (100m) affects classification granularity

Publication Venues

  • Journal of Coastal Research - beach classification methods
  • Geomorphology - morphodynamic systems
  • Marine Turtle Newsletter - management application

Complete Implementation Summary

Primary Research Question

"Which beach segments at PAIS demonstrate temporal stability in geomorphic characteristics critical for Kemp's ridley nesting (slope, elevation, beach width), and can a Morphological Stability Index predict persistent vs. ephemeral high-quality habitat zones?"

Three Core Objectives

  1. Quantify temporal variability in key habitat parameters (elevation, slope, beach width) across 2024-2026 MLS time series
  2. Develop Composite Stability Index (CSI) integrating multiple geomorphic metrics to classify beach segments by stability
  3. Map persistent vs. ephemeral habitat zones and correlate with Kemp's ridley nest distribution patterns

Expected Deliverables

  • 6 maps: Individual stability metrics (elevation, slope, width, change magnitude, dune toe migration) + final CSI map
  • Persistent Habitat Map: Zones maintaining suitability in all 3 years vs. intermittent vs. ephemeral
  • Conservation Priority Map: 4-tier classification for management action
  • Statistical analysis: Nest density comparison across stability classes (ANOVA/Kruskal-Wallis)
  • Management recommendations: Segment rankings for protection priority

Timeline (within your MS program)

  • Fall 2024: Literature review, methodology development, process 2024 MLS data
  • Spring 2025: Process 2025 MLS data, preliminary 2-year analysis
  • Summer 2025: Participate in 2026 RA field campaign (MLS + RTK surveys)
  • Fall 2025: Process 2026 MLS data, complete 3-year stability analysis
  • Spring 2026: Finalize analysis, thesis writing, defense

Alternative Directions Worth Considering

Direction 1 (Score 21) is excellent if you want maximum novelty and publication impact (microtopography is completely new). However, it requires high-quality nest GPS data and more advanced texture analysis.

Direction 4 (Score 21) is ideal if you want to focus on climate change projections and future scenarios. Produces highly relevant SLR adaptation tools but assumes linear change rates (more uncertainty).

Final Recommendation: Direction 2 offers the best balance of novelty, feasibility, management relevance, and alignment with your RA project timeline and data. It positions you for a strong thesis defense and potential peer-reviewed publication while providing PAIS with immediately usable conservation tools.
